On July 15, iCrowd announced new AEO and GEO services bundled into its press release distribution product, pitched as a way to get brands "discovered inside AI-generated answers" when journalists and models pull from wire content. The announcement ran through The Globe and Mail's newswire channel, which is standard for vendor launches in this category. It landed the same week Frostbite Marketing, Columbus Marketing Experts, and The Enough Agency all pushed their own SEO-plus-AEO service lines into the news cycle.

That clustering matters. When four unrelated vendors ship nearly identical positioning inside seventy-two hours, you are usually looking at a category land grab, not four independent breakthroughs.

## What does an "AEO press release" actually mean?

An AEO press release, in practice, is still a press release: a dated announcement, written in third person, distributed through a wire or syndication network, with links back to a landing page. Vendors add GEO or AEO language when they rewrite the headline stack, inject FAQ-style subheads, or tune the boilerplate for entity clarity (company name, product category, geography, executive titles). Some also promise placement on domains that AI systems crawl frequently.

That can help humans and models understand *who you are* and *what you announced*. It does not, by itself, guarantee that ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity will cite you the next time someone asks a category question without naming your brand.

## Should you pay for AI-optimized wire distribution?

Maybe, if your goal is legitimate news distribution and you already have something newsworthy to say: a product launch, a funding round, a data report, a partnership. Wire services still put announcements in front of journalists, bloggers, and aggregators. Clear writing and consistent entity naming make those pickups easier to parse.

It is a bad buy if you expect the wire package to substitute for product documentation, customer proof, or the slow work of earning third-party coverage. Google's generative search guidance, which we covered on this site on July 6, explicitly warns against chasing inauthentic mentions. A burst of syndicated releases that all say the same thing about your "AI visibility platform" reads less like authority and more like coordinated noise.

## How is this different from what Coozmoo and Frostbite are selling?

Coozmoo's launch emphasized answer-engine visibility inside conversational products and a RankRabbit-style tracking dashboard. Frostbite's pitch combines classic SEO retainers with bundled AEO and GEO language. iCrowd sits upstream of both: it is distribution infrastructure, not a strategy shop. That makes the offer more honest in one sense (you are buying reach, not magic) and more dangerous in another, because cheap distribution makes it tempting to spam marginal "news" and call it optimization.

The Enough Agency's citation-services expansion, also out this week, is the measurement flip side of the same coin. Everyone wants to sell you the dials; fewer want to tell you that the dial often moves because your underlying facts got clearer, not because someone rewrote your press release boilerplate.

## What should a white-hat team do instead?

Start with one verifiable announcement worth reading, not twelve variations of "we are now an AI-first brand." Put the same facts on your site, your GitHub org profile if you are devtools, your app store listing, and your About page, using identical product names and dates. Publish primary documentation that matches what the release claims. Then distribute once, track pickups, and fix inconsistencies when you find them.

If you need a baseline read on whether AI systems already mention you for category queries, run that check before you spend on wire upgrades. An AI-search research tool like [Searchable](aff://searchable) gives you a visibility snapshot across major assistants without locking you into a monthly syndication bundle.

**TLDR:** iCrowd's AEO press release tier is distribution with clearer entity writing, not a shortcut into model citations. Use wires for real news; do not use them to manufacture the appearance of authority.
