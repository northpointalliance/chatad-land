CiteLens, a small AI-visibility platform, published a benchmark on July 8 that's worth more attention than it got. The company ran 320 real buyer queries through ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google's AI Mode, then compared every source each engine cited against the same query's Google and Bing organic results. The gap between engines was not small.

Google's own AI Mode pulled 93% of its citations from Google's top-10 organic results. Perplexity pulled 89%. For both, the correlation between Google ranking and citation frequency came out near 0.9, statistically about as tight as these things get. Claude was different: only 53% of its citations came from Google's top-10, and its picks tracked brand recognition more than ranking, with 58% going to sites that have a Wikipedia presence. ChatGPT was the outlier. Only 30% of its citations matched Google's top-10, correlation with ranking was near zero, and only 21% went to Wikipedia-backed sites. Fewer than 4% of ChatGPT's citations even showed up in Bing's top-10, which rules out the easy explanation that it's just quietly running on Microsoft's index.

## Take the number, question the frame

Two things are true at once here. The data is genuinely useful, and the source has an obvious incentive to make GEO sound like three separate disciplines instead of one, because CiteLens sells the dashboard that tracks all three. The sample is also narrower than the headline suggests: 320 queries across three consumer sectors in the Turkish market, not a broad multi-market study. Directionally interesting, not a universal law.

With that caveat priced in, the finding still lines up with something worth taking seriously: there is no single "rank here, get cited everywhere" move. Optimizing for Google AI Mode and Perplexity looks a lot like optimizing for Google itself, because both are still leaning on the search index as the entry gate. Optimizing for ChatGPT looks like something closer to earning general entity authority across the web, independent of where you rank on any one search engine.

## This is also why the press release approach half-works

We covered iCrowd's AEO press release push on this site last week and were skeptical that syndication alone buys you AI citations. This data explains the mechanism better than we could last week. A wire release that gets picked up and indexed can absolutely help you with Google AI Mode and Perplexity, because those two engines are still substantially ranking-driven. It does close to nothing for ChatGPT, because ChatGPT isn't rewarding index position at all. If your AEO plan is "get more pickups," you're optimizing for two engines out of four and assuming the other two will follow. They won't.

## What to actually do with this

Don't build one GEO checklist. Build two tracks: a ranking-and-crawlability track for Google AI Mode and Perplexity, and a brand-authority track (consistent naming, third-party mentions, a real Wikipedia or equivalent presence) for Claude and ChatGPT. If you want your own numbers instead of trusting a vendor's press release about its own product, run your brand through an AI-search visibility tool like [Searchable](aff://searchable) and see where the actual gap sits before you plan a quarter of content around one engine's behavior.

**TLDR:** CiteLens's data, self-interested source and small sample aside, points to a real split: Google AI Mode and Perplexity still reward SEO, Claude rewards brand recognition, and ChatGPT rewards neither in any measurable way. Plan for three different games, not one.
